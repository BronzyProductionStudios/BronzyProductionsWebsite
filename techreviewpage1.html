Falcon Heavy, The Worlds Most Powerful Rocket,

Moses Huf-Tirfe, June 14th, 2018

In early February of this year, history was changed. I know it sounds cliche but it has truth to it. See the future of space travel is reusability everyone knows that. SpaceX was able to significantly cut ULA's (United Launch Alliance (Boeing and Lockheed joint venture)) cost simply by reusing their boosters instead of dumping them in the Pacific or Atlantic (Often the Atlantic) and expecting a heavy payroll.

But even though the reusable and reliable Falcon 9 (Three of which are used to make the first stage of the Falcon Heavy) booster had been flown over 40 times as of February no one believed a reusable rocket could get much past Low Earth Orbit. But in February the Falcon Heavy did just that and is bound for a flyby of Mars.

It's Awe Inspiring


I was given the privilege of watching the Falcon Heavy in person and for someone who has only seen a couple of rocket launches in his life (most of which were small sounding rockets), this was a great privilege. People knew this would be a launch like no other. There were people crammed on the side of the road and in the streets and on the beach.

LIFT OFF!


And when it lifted off it had a level of strength unseen since the shuttle (Retired 7 years prior) and even the mighty Saturn V (Last launched in 1973) and a payload capacity more than the shuttle and only a couple tons off the Saturn V's record.

The landing of the twin side boosters was a great success with the almost simultaneous landing. However, the only real failure of the entire mission was the miscalculation of the landing of the center stage which missed its landing barge and plummeted into the ocean. Yet for all intensive purposes, this rocket was a success.

But this is simply a stepping stone. There will be more but in the meantime, this is a great step forward. For all humankind.






Eutropia are a team compiled of 5 Australian Year-10 students who are developing the worlds first reusable hybrid rocket system.

Right now it costs twenty-two thousand dollars to take one kilogram of payload into low earth orbit. After two launches we will be able to take one kilogram of payload into low earth orbit for as little as one thousand dollars.
